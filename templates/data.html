Data Analysis Technical Build Team Lead

Over the course of my Fall 2020 semester at UT, I served as a technical build team lead in Texas Convergent. Convergent is an interdisciplinary coding organization that teaches students to build their startup ideas from the ground up. The organization is broken up into several build teams with each focusing on a central theme. My role was on the Data Analysis build team, where data processing and modeling were core to each project. Throughout the semester, I remotely mentored three student subteams to ideate, design, and implement data science projects.

Responsibilities

As a lead, one of my responsibilities was to build out the technical curriculum with lectures and tutorials. My goal was to ensure students not only understood how to implement data analysis technically but also why each step is vital. To accomplish this, I prepared several presentations to cover subjects from obtaining and cleaning data to drawing insights through models and visualization. I also led code walkthroughs to teach the tools students would need for their projects. For example, I introduced data preprocessing with a tutorial covering the basics of Python and the pandas library. This involved operating on an example dataset and processing out unnecessary, duplicated, or missing information while demonstrating useful pandas functions. The focus for these tutorials was to provide students with the skeleton of how to implement data analysis rather than a strict set of steps to follow. After lectures and tutorials, subteams would break out to work within their groups.

Mentoring the student subteams directly made up the most rewarding and challenging part of my role. During breakouts, I gave students the specific help they needed for their respective projects. For the early stages of their projects, I largely gave advice on how they should best design their projects. Since many students were new to working with data, I focused on getting them to think about what valuable information a dataset might hold. If a dataset looked remotely related to their project, I advised they investigate it in detail and see if any of its insights might be useful to their project. From there it was making myself a resource for the subteams as they started implementing their designs. Much of this mentoring was done by students coding while sharing their screens with the team. While it was not the most optimal format, livesharing code did come with some benefits. For example, every student could see the important areas of the code be written. This meant that rather than splitting off to work on different areas, each student understood how the core parts of their project operated. Since these core areas are where most of the data science help was needed, most students could cement their knowledge of working with data this way. Seeing my students improve not only their technical ability but also their understanding of data science was incredibly rewarding. Despite the challenges posed by teaching remotely, I believe my work in the breakouts ensured students were engaged and learning. Overall, the breakouts allowed me to give direct technical assistance and teach the right tools the subteams needed for their projects.

Subteam Projects

At the beginning of the course, students proposed several project ideas that were narrowed down to three that would be built over the semester. Each student subteam built a business plan and minimum viable product off of these ideas. Descriptions and links to their presentations are below:

autoClips

This is the subteam I worked the closest with and I am really proud of their progress over the semester. At first, this team wanted to build an application to determine which basketball games are worth tuning into. After reviewing what data sources were available and what they could do with that data, they shifted to building a model that can automatically highlight the best plays of any NFL game. The basic proposal is that, rather than using the time of editors, producers, and directors to produce highlight clips, a data-based algorithm could do it unsupervised. By replacing the middlemen in this process, autoClips cuts down the price required to produce these clips.

The team used an NFL dataset containing information on 390,000 individual plays over 10 seasons to build their model. This dataset contained information on the type of play, yards gained, penalties, calculated win percentage, and much more. Because their project needed to highlight the best plays from a game, they needed a method to calculate how exciting any given play was. The formula they used was the Game Excitement Index. Essentially, the GEI can be used to calculate how exciting any given play is by highlighting large win percentage swings normalized for game length. During a live game, it is not possible to have access to this win percentage data. Therefore, the win percentage is not considered in the training or testing set. The goal here was to build a machine learning model that could calculate a GEI for any play based on that play’s data rather than difference in win percentage. Several machine learning models were tried that produced interesting results, but I’d like to highlight their Bayesian Ridge Regression model. The graph below is plotted with actual GEI on the x-axis and predicted GEI on the y-axis.

This Bayesian Ridge Regression model was trained on 377,193 plays and tested on a random game’s 139 plays. As can be seen above, the model is certainly not perfect as many plays deemed “exciting” in the training set are not predicted as such. One possibility of this accuracy is that the model looks at each play discreetly and somewhat lacks the ability to read comebacks or turnarounds. However, the plays this model rates as the most exciting are consistently quite accurate.

Overall, the minimum viable product delivered by this team showed promising results. While there are some issues that could be smoothed out with a more polished model, the current iteration can consistently identify especially exciting plays. I also learned a lot by working closely with this team to help with any design and implementation issues.

Moodify

Moodify is a platform that generates Spotify playlists based on mood. Generally, mood playlists offered by Spotify are pre-curated so they cannot take personal taste into account. If you want to make a mood playlist with the songs you love, it can take a while to add them all manually. Moodify streamlines this process by selecting the best-fitting songs from your other playlists to create mood playlists.

Moodify’s implementation is based all around audio data. The process breaks songs down into audio features such as loudness, tempo, and energy. Each song has measurable levels for each audio feature. From there, the team analyzed which features correspond to certain moods. As can be seen above, a workout playlist will have high danceability and energy but low acousticness. On the other hand, a sad playlist will have lower energy but high acousticness instead. Overall, Moodify leverages audio data to improve the experience of creating mood playlists.

GenerEAT

GenerEAT creates recipe ideas and meal plans according to BMI and taste/macro preferences. For many people, finding meals that are both healthy and tasty can be a challenge. GenerEAT aims to use recipe and meal data to provide the best options that meet a user’s preferences and nutritional goals.

This project uses a large meal dataset that includes both detailed recipes and comprehensive nutritional information. From there, users can receive auto generated meal ideas based on caloric intake and nutritional value. Users can set preferences for vegetarian, high protein, or low fat meals to further improve the results they get. The preferences also extend to taste so users can find the perfect recipes for them. GenerEAT fixes the inconvenience of manual meal planning by taking a data-based approach to what people eat.

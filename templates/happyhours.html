Happy Hours

During my Fall 2019 semester, I was a member on Texas Convergent’s Data Analysis build team. This was my first experience building a data science project and really started my interest in that field. My subteam’s goal was to build a data product that could improve the restaurant industry. The problem we chose to focus on was that empty tables don’t make money. That is, when restaurants have open seating, they are missing out on potential profit. Our solution was to build Happy Hours.

Happy Hours is a model that recommends restaurant discounts based on popularity and sales data. Throughout the day, any restaurant has “on” hours and “off” hours in terms of getting customers. The goal of the Happy Hours discounts is to drive up business during those off hours. If the gap between the on and off hours can be narrowed, restaurants can generate profit during times where they usually don’t get business.

Once we finalized the scope and idea behind our project, one of my first responsibilities was to analyze restaurant’s sales data. This would give us some idea into how big the gap between on and off hours really was. Using restaurant takeout sales data taken from Kaggle, I charted the number of sales respective to day of the week and time of day. Every sales dataset I processed in this way showed us a massive disparity between on and off hours.

With the premise of our project confirmed, we began creating a model to determine which discounts could effectively drive business during off hours. One model my team considered was the price elasticity of demand. This formula states a 1% drop in price can lead to a 2.3% increase in business. While this formula could be a good starting place, I wanted to compare how discounts work in practice. Using Google Maps popularity data from several restaurants that regularly offer discounts, I charted how business is affected during these times. While many factors play into restaurant sales, the graphs showed the restaurants with consistent discounts had a smaller difference between a restaurant’s peak and average popularity.

From there, our project utilized the price elasticity of demand across a restaurant’s sales data to recommend discounts. By the end of the semester, we delivered a prototype of Happy Hours that could recommend effective discounts based on historical and predicted sales. This project really introduced me to data science and especially how to operate on datasets in Python. I learned a lot in this course, and it encouraged me to eventually take on the role of Data Analysis technical lead in Convergent.
